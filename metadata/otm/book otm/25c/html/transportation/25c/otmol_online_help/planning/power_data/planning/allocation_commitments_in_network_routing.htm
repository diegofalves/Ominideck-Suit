Allocation Commitments in Network Routing

OTM planning can consider service provider percentage allocation commitments when making routing selections for orders. This is enabled via the network routing logic configuration parameter USE CARRIER COMMITMENTS IN ROUTE SELECTION. The parameter CARRIER COMMITMENT PENALTY FOR ROUTE SELECTION can be used to set a penalty.

Note: Only allocations on primary legs are considered in network routing. The parameter APPLY ALLOCATION GROUP ON PRIMARY LEG ONLY does not affect this.

In a multi-leg transportation network, different carriers may be associated with different intermediate locations. The selection of a certain carrier may determine which intermediate locations are used; conversely, the selection of intermediate points may limit the choice of carrier.

An example is the selection of port pairs in a land-ocean-land scenario. Different ocean service providers may sail out of or into different ports. For example, one service provider (carrier A) might only sail out of Port of Long Beach while another service provider (carrier B) might only sail out of the nearby Port of Los Angeles. An order will be routed into one port or the other depending on which service provider is to be used.

Often, order routing decisions are based upon cost, so the rates of carriers A and B are used to determine whether to route through Long Beach or through Los Angeles. However, you may have a contract with the service providers whereby a certain percentage (e.g., 60%) of shipments from the Long Beach / Los Angeles area are to go to carrier A and a certain percentage (e.g., 40%) to carrier B. In this case, if OTM is making routing decisions purely based on cost, it will not fulfill the allocation commitments. OTM can make the routing decision between Long Beach and Los Angeles based upon the relevant allocation percentages. In this case, OTM should route the orders so that 60% of the shipments are on carrier A out of Long Beach, and 40% of the shipments are on carrier B out of Los Angeles.

Transportation Network Scenario

There are two possible routes:

  • Source -> Baltimore Cross-dock -> Baltimore Port -> Â (Carrier A) Le Havre Port -> Le Havre Cross-dock -> Paris
  • Source -> Baltimore Cross-dock -> Baltimore Port -> Carrier B) Brest Port -> Brest Cross-dock -> Paris

Carrier commitments:

  • Carrier A - 60% - offers shipments on 20ft containers (FEU = 0.5)
  • Carrier B - 40% - offers shipments on 40ft containers (FEU = 1) - making this cheaper overall.

Orders are bulk planned using a parameter set with Network Routing turned on. Each order fills one 20ft container; two orders can fit in a 40ft container.

Use Case 1 - No Commitment Constraints

OTM bulk plans several orders through this network. All of the orders are planned through the cheaper route; through Brest.

Use Case 2 - Commitment Constraints by Shipment

Allocation commitment constraints are set up on the US port - French ports lane:

  • Carrier A: 60%
  • Carrier B: 40%

These commitments are by shipment.

Without the commitment constraints, all orders would be planned on Carrier B through the Brest port. With these commitments, however, OTM should plan 60% of the ocean shipments on Carrier A through Le Havre. For example, if 14 orders are being planned, the correct result would include 6 shipments (6 orders) on Carrier A to the Le Havre port, and 4 shipments (8 orders) on Carrier B to the Brest port (and the corresponding drayage and delivery shipments).

The commitment constraints should be satisfied cumulatively over the commitment allocation period. That is, OTM should try to assign service providers so as to meet the commitment allocation constraints within the current period (day, week, month, etc), rather than just for the given bulk plan. So, in the previous example, if there were already 6 shipments (6 orders) on Carrier A to Le Havre, and none on Carrier B to Brest, a bulk plan of 8 orders should result in 4 shipments (8 orders) on Carrier B to Brest, in order to balance the overall allocation to 60%/40%.

Use Case 3 - Commitment Constraints by FEU

In this use case, as before, allocation commitment constraints are set up on the US port - French ports lane:

  • Carrier A: 60%
  • Carrier B: 40%

However, these commitments are by FEU rather than shipment.

With these commitments, however, OTM should plan 60% of the ocean-going FEUs on Carrier A through Le Havre, and 40% on Carrier B through Brest. For example, when 10 orders are planned, 6 orders (6 shipments = 3 FEU) should go on Carrier A to Le Havre, and 4 orders (2 shipments = 2 FEU) should go on Carrier B to Brest.

Use Case 4 - Commitment Constraints with Single Shipment

Again the allocation commitment constraints are set up on the US port - French ports lane:

  • Carrier A: 50%
  • Carrier B: 50%

These commitments are by shipment.

In this use case, there are two small orders that can fit into the same 20ft container. When these two orders are planned together, OTM should plan both through the cheaper path, on Carrier B through Brest.

They should be planned together even though to do so gives Carrier B 100% of the shipments (i.e., one out of one) and Carrier A 0%. The orders could have been routed separately, one on Carrier A and one on Carrier B, so that each carrier has its allocation commitment satisfied at 50%, but to do so would make no sense from a business point of view.
